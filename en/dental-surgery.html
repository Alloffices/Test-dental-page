Dentalsurgery

Extraction

Tooth extraction is the most common surgical procedure. There are numerous reasons why a tooth needs to be removed.

  • Deciduous teeth (or baby teeth) should normally remain in their place until the permanent teeth appear. However, a large decay or an accident may necessitate an early extraction. Sometimes orthodontists suggest an extraction in order to create more favourable circumstances for the erupting permanent teeth.
  • Permanent teeth are less simple cases. Because of their longer multiple roots and the harder bone around them, they are fixed in the jaw more firmly. Some of the possible causes that can lead to an extraction:
    • extensive decay
    • inflammation of the supporting tissues of the tooth (periodontitis)
    • inflammation of the tooth pulp
    • a cyst has developed around the tooth
    • the tooth is broken along its axis
  • Wisdom teeth can be quite difficult to remove. Their irregular position in the jaw as well as their crooked roots often call for a surgical extraction (osteotomy). This process involves an incision in the gum and the removal of the overlying bone tissue. The impacted the tooth may be split into multiple pieces to facilitate its removal.

Root tip removal (apicectomy)

Whenever the infection of a tooth has travelled beyond the root tip (the apex) into the bone, and not even a repeated root canal treatment is expected to solve the problem, an apicectomy might be performed to avoid an extraction and thus save the tooth. During this procedure the covering gum and bone are cut and lifted to gain access to the root. Its tip is then removed along with the infected tissue and the gum is eventually stitched back into place.

Cystectomy

An oral cyst is an abnormal sac formed anywhere in the mouth containing gas or liquid substance. They are generally painless and smaller than 1 inch, not even noticed until they become large enough to cause a bulge. The most common kinds are periapical cysts, caused by infections in the pulp of the tooth, and odontogenic cysts usually formed by impacted wisdom teeth.

While these are benign, dentists generally recommend removal because they tend to grow and can weaken or break the jaw and damage surrounding teeth. Cyst removal is mostly done under local anaesthesia with a single surgical procedure. The remaining cavity can be filled with bone graft to promote the formation of new bone.
