Dentalrestorations

Crowns

Crowns are the preferred way of restoration for teeth that have been broken, or have been weakened by decay or a very large filling. They are also referred to as a 'cap' that fits over the remaining part of the prepared tooth covering it and thus improving both its strength and appearance. To make a crown needs 2-3 appointments depending on the complexity of the case.

In today's dentistry, the most often used types of crowns are as follows:

  • Porcelain-fused-to-metal (PFM) crowns are considered the standard solution because of their satisfactory durability and aesthetics. Their acceptable price makes them an optimal choice in most cases.
  • All-porcelain crowns present the best aesthetic results in the front region. Their optical characteristics are very close to those of natural teeth. Not even a dentist can distinguish them at first sight.
  • Zirconia crowns are probably the most significant invention in modern prosthodontics on the path towards metal-free restorations. The reason why they have become so popular is their longevity and excellent aesthetics. Since they contain no metal they are highly biocompatible, too.
  • Procera crowns are the latest development in dentistry. They are fabricated by a revolutionary new process through wich an exceptionally high precision can be reached. These high-tech crowns offer the best quality available today which is not necessarily reflected in their price.
  • Veneers belong to a special class of crowns. You can learn more about them in the section Aesthetic dentistry.

Bridges

The simplest way to close gaps in the dental arch is with the use of bridges. The pontic replaces the missing tooth while the anchoring crowns join the entire structure permanently on the adjacent teeth. Fixed bridges are not only more comfortable to wear than removable dentures, they also last longer and help preserve anatomical structures. That's why they are preferred to other alternatives. Average time span of fabrication: 3 visits within 7-9 working days.

Typical sorts of bridges:

  • Porcelain-fused-to-metal (PFM) bridges consist of a metal framework that provides the needed compressive and tensile strength, and a porcelain coating that gives the bridge its tooth-like appearance.
  • Zirconia bridges are metal-free. They contain a tooth-coloured framework made of zirconia, an extremely hard type of ceramic, and the usual porcelain coating. They not only look better than regular PFM bridges, they even last longer.
  • Procera bridges can be made out of any material of choice without limitation on their extent. Thus, they offer the freedom to choose the best suitable combination and the highest available quality at the same time.
  • Inlay bridges only differ from others in that they are anchored to the adjacent teeth with inlays instead of crowns. Although this way the construction is less stable, considerably more healthy tooth enamel can be saved.
  • Telescopic bridges possess the advantages of both fixed bridges and removable dentures.

Removable partial dentures

Whenever too many teeth are missing, and the number and/or condition of the remaining ones do not allow the application of a fixed bridge, a removable denture is made instead. These wider gaps can be closed with a partial denture joined to the remaining teeth. This way it is possible to convey the load onto them and to prevent further bone loss.

Partial dentures are complicated devices. Their fabrication takes 4-5 visits within 10 working days at least. The numerous variations differ from each other in the way they are attached to the teeth:

  • Dentures with clasps may loosen the anchoring teeth and cause alveolar bone loss. Their appearance is not too attractive either. They are only recommended as temporary dentures.
  • Cast metal base dentures are similar devices but contain stronger metallic anchoring elements and thus they are more stable and less harmful than the above but still not the ideal dental restoration.
  • Dentures with precision attachment consist of a fixed part, usually a bridge, and a removable part. The attachment elements can be of several different types, though they all have one thing in common: during function they convey the load - at least partially - onto the healthy teeth preventing immediate bone loss.
  • Telescopic dentures are commonly used when the remaining teeth are either too few or too loose. Should any of them be lost any time later the denture can easily be adjusted to the new situation which is an invaluable advantage.
  • Telescopic bridges are a similar construction except that the visible frontal part (or sometimes the whole denture) is reduced and designed as a bridge in order to make it more aesthetic and comfortable.

Full dentures

When every tooth is missing a removable full denture is the standard treatment. In such a case other parts of the mouth must help to hold the denture in place. However, not even an edentulous jaw is a hopeless case. Using implants the stability improves remarkably.

  • Conventional full dentures only sit on the gum and the surrounding soft and loose mucosa. The conditions of retention are, of course, not very favourable, escpecially in the lower jaw.
  • Implant supported dentures are the ultimate solution. Even in old age and with receded jaw bone there is almost always enough bone substance to hold 4 implants. The stability achieved in this manner is excellent, not even comparable with that of conventional dentures.
